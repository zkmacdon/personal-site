University of Ottawa

May — Sept. 2024

Research Assistant

Under Professor Arthur Mehta in the Department of Mathematics, I spent the summer studying and experimenting with non-local games. In particular, working alongisde a graduate student and fellow undergraduate, we explored variants of the CHSH game. Some of that work looked like:

  • Reading notes on the mathematical foundations of quantum computing and papers pertaining to variants of the CHSH game. This involved learning about tensors, higher order functions, and lots more — particularly in the context of quantum computing.
  • Running 1000's of simulations of the multiplicative variants of the standard, mod-2 CHSH game as generated from roots of unity. Done in Python, this required the use of IBM's Qiskit library as well as numpy, sympy, and others to encode the components of the quantum circuits and run the simulations.
  • Preparing slides to for a presentation about quantum computing for undergaduate science students. The presentation showed how the CHSH game proves the legitimacy of quantum advantage.
It was a fascinating experience, and am thankful to have been exposed to higher-level mathematics and explore its applications in quantum computing!

Collatz Numbers

In this project, I took the well-known Collatz conjecture from mathematics and used recursion to model it. I then played around with the behaviour some more, and upon visualising the behaviour of Collatz Numbers (a term I came up with to describe the number of iterations it takes for the algorithm to reach 1), I discovered the existence of "Collatz Clusters" -- consecutive numbers which share the same Collatz Number.
